The difference between **brown** and **brownish** comes down to certainty and purity. It is the difference between a definitive color and a color that is "close enough" or mixed with something else.
Here is a simple breakdown of how they differ in everyday language:
### 1. Definitive vs. Approximate
 * **Brown:** This is absolute. When you call something brown, you are stating that it is fully and clearly that color (e.g., *a brown leather jacket*, *brown dirt*).
 * **Brownish:** The suffix **"-ish"** means "approximately" or "resembling." You use brownish when you aren't entirely sure, or when the color is only *mostly* brown (e.g., *"His jacket was a brownish color"* means it might be brown, or maybe a dark tan, or maroon).
### 2. Pure vs. Mixed Tints
 * **Brown:** Refers to the distinct color family itself, without focusing on undertones.
 * **Brownish:** Usually implies that brown is the dominant color, but it is blended with another hue.
   * *Greenish-brownish* (brown with a hint of green).
   * *Reddish-brownish* (brown with a hint of red).
### Comparison Summary
| Feature | Brown | Brownish |
|---|---|---|
| **Certainty** | Exact and direct | Evaluative or approximate |
| **Composition** | A solid, primary description | Often a blend of brown and another undertone |
| **Common Use** | Standard naming (*"brown hair"*) | Describing natural, varied tones (*"brownish leaves"*) |
In short, if a color is undeniably brown, it is **brown**. If it just leans toward brown, or is a mix of brown and something else, it is **brownish**.
